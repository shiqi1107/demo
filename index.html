A Digital Dream

As computers, media and telecommunication technology continue to collect, manipulate, store, represent and transmit an ever-increasing flux of data, they are installing nothing less than a new dimension: the space of information.

This multi-dimensional space is virtual, densely webbed, and infinitely complex — a realm accessed only through the mediation of our imaginative and technical representations.

How powerfully we engage it depends on how powerfully we both manipulate and inhabit these ghost-like phantasms of the interface.

Through the Liquid Crystal

Things do not work the same through the liquid crystal looking-glass, with its codes, hypertexts, simulated spaces, labyrinthine network architectures, baroque metaphors, and colossal encyclopedias of memory.

Information theory mutates into praxis: how do we move through this space? What logics, cartographies, entities, connections emerge?

Interface designers are already grappling with the phantasmic apparatus of imagination — a challenge for both dreaming and analytic minds.

Information Under Pressure

Far beyond Silicon Valley, phantasms hover over the technologically-mediated processing that increasingly shapes our world.

The word "information" crackles with pressure — drawing mythologies, metaphysics, even arcane magic into its orbit.

Imaginative Constructs

"Science fiction has already explored such mythologies of information..."

The constructs that emerge — William Gibson's Cyberspace, Vinge's Other Plane, and Dick's VALIS — are not just fiction.

They are mobile ideas, circulating and evolving in our digital culture. Each serves as a kind of magical interface between imagination, language, and the technological unconscious.

Hermetic Imagination

"Magicians hoarded a stunning amount of information: ritual names, talismanic images, and gnostic symbols — all used to capture astral forces."

From trickster tales to mystical conceptions of the Logos, the hermetic imagination maps the obscure. Its methods include demonic cryptography, the art of memory, and gnostic cosmology.

These practices were not metaphorical — they were techniques of knowledge control, embedded in sigils, numbers, rituals, and sacred lists.
